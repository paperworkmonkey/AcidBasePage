Metabolic alkalosis

  • Chloride loss:
    • GIT - vomiting, NG aspiration, vilous adenoma
    • Renal: Chloriuretic diuretics, severe K depletion, post-hypercapnia
    • Skin: cystic fibrosis
  • Potassium loss:
    • GIT: laxative abuse
    • Renal: hyperaldosteronism (primary and secondary), other hypokalaemic hypertensive syndromes (incl Bartter adn Gitelman)
  • Base loading in presence of low GFR
    • Milk-alkali syndrome
    • Base loading in ESRF
    • Co-administration of nonreabsorbable antacids and cation-exchange resin
  • Miscellaneous
    • post-hypercapnic states
    • Multiple citrate blood transfusions
    • widespread bont metastases
    • recovery from starvation

Respiratory alkalosis

  • CNS stimulation
    • pain, anxiety
    • ischaemia
    • stroke
    • tumour
    • infeciton
    • drugs (salicylates, progesterone, stimulants (doxapram))
  • Peripheral stimulation
    • hypoxaemia
    • altitude
    • pulmonary disease (congestive HF, non-cardiogenic pulmonary oedema, asthma, PE)
    • severe anaemia
  • Unknown mechanism
    • sepsis
    • metabolic encephalopathies

Delta values

  • Delta Ratio less than 1: Suggests a normal anion gap metabolic acidosis (NAGMA).
  • Delta Ratio > 2: Suggests a concurrent metabolic alkalosis or pre-existing high bicarbonate.

CO32- Carbonate
HA Weak acid
A- Weak anions

  • SIDa

    SIDa = apparent SID = charge difference between measures strong ions and strong anions. Normal values 40-46. SIDa = (Na+ + K+ + Ca2+ + Mg2+) - (Cl− + Lactate−)

  • SIDe

    SIDe = Effective SID = calculated sum of charges measured weak acids in plasma (bic, albumin and phosphate).
    SIDe = Bic- + Albumin- + Phosphate-
    Normal value is 36-40.

  • SIG

    SIG = SIDa – SIDe. i.e. take the SIDa (calculated from difference in charges on strong cations and anions) and subtract the charges from weak acids (SIDe) - what is left is a caused by known anions (e.g. sulphate, acetate, citrate, D-lactate etc.).
    • Increased SIG acidosis:
      1. ketoacidosis
      2. D-lactate
      3. uraemia
      4. hepatic failure
      5. toxins ( (organic alcohols, iron, isoniazid, cyanide paraldehyde, salicylates))
    • Decreased SIG acidosis:
      1. lithium toxicity
      2. bromine toxicity
      3. THAM
      4. paraproteins

Stewart equations

  1. [H+] × [OH−] = K′w (water dissociation equilibrium)
  2. [H+] × [A−] = KA × [HA] (weak acid equilibrium)
  3. [HA] + [A−] = [ATOT] (conservation of mass for “A”)
  4. [H+] × [HCO3−] = KC × pCO2 (bicarbonate ion formation equilibrium)
  5. [H+] × [CO32−] = K3 × [HCO3−] (carbonate ion formation equilibrium)
  6. [SID] + [H+] − [HCO3−] − [A−] − [CO32−] − [OH−] = 0 (maintenance of electrical neutrality)
